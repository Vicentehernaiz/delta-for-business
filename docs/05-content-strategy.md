# 05 — Content Strategy & Tone of Voice
## Delta for Business Web Redesign

---

## Core message hierarchy (from Figma flow)

Every page answers in this order:
1. **WHAT** — what is this program
2. **FOR WHO** — exact size / spend / role
3. **HOW** — how it works mechanically
4. **BENEFITS FOR COMPANY** — what the company gets
5. **BENEFITS FOR EMPLOYEES** — what individuals get
6. **AVAILABLE CARDS** — which Amex cards work with this
7. **MILES POLITICS** — how miles are earned and used

---

## Tone of voice

**Premium but approachable.** Delta's corporate audience is CFOs, travel managers, and executive assistants — not casual consumers. Write like a trusted advisor, not a salesperson.

**Clarity over cleverness.** The biggest UX problem today is confusion between the three programs. Every word should reduce confusion, not add to it.

**Specific over vague.** "Up to 10 miles per dollar" beats "earn more miles." "$2,500 MQD Headstart" beats "jump-start your status."

**Active voice.** "Your company earns miles" not "Miles are earned by your company."

**Acknowledge the complexity, then simplify it.** Users know corporate travel is complicated. Acknowledge it briefly, then immediately solve it.

---

## Key phrases to use

- "Find your program in 60 seconds" (quiz entry)
- "Your company earns miles. Your employees earn miles. Same flight." (double-earn)
- "Optimized for managers" (mid-market + enterprise positioning)
- "No minimum spend" (individual program)
- "Free enrollment" (SMB + individual)
- "Contact sales" (mid-market + enterprise — never "buy" or "sign up")
- "Negotiated fares" (not "discounts" — sounds premium)
- "Corporate Priority" (always with capital letters — it's a proper product name)

---

## Key phrases to avoid

- "Corporate program" without specifying which one
- "Points" — Delta uses "miles" exclusively
- "Loyalty program" — too generic, say the program name
- "Discount" for enterprise — say "negotiated fares"
- "Sign up" for enterprise — say "contact sales" or "get a proposal"
- Jargon without explanation: MQD, MSA, CSA, IATA — always explain first use

## Value propositions — one line per segment

These are the distilled, honest propositions that every page, CTA, and piece of copy should reinforce.

**Individual Business Traveler:**
"Descuentos en servicios de viaje que probablemente ya usas, más la puerta de entrada al ecosistema Delta para cuando tu empresa quiera hacer más."
→ English: *"Discounts on travel services you probably already use, plus the entry door to Delta's ecosystem for when your company wants to do more."*
→ **Honest positioning:** This is a funnel acquisition tool, not a standalone product. No flight benefits. Partner perks only (Hertz Five Star, CLEAR Plus, Industrious $99/mo, LinkedIn Premium). Present it as the **first step** — "start here if it's just you" — without overstating what it delivers. Users will detect exaggeration immediately.
→ **Unique competitive position:** Delta is the only major US airline with any individual traveler program. Zero competition. But zero flight benefit too.

**Small Business (SkyMiles for Business):**
"Tu empresa y tus empleados ganan millas en el mismo vuelo, al mismo tiempo, sin que uno le quite al otro."
→ English: *"Your company and your employees earn miles on the same flight, at the same time, without one reducing the other."*
→ **The insight:** Double-earn is the central mechanic. Company earns on fare base, employee earns personal SkyMiles + MQDs with no reduction. Two accounts, one ticket, zero trade-off. This resolves the classic corporate conflict — the employee doesn't lose personal benefits when forced to fly a specific airline.
→ **Secondary value:** Company miles convert to real, tangible value — eCredits (~1.5–2¢/mile), transfers to employee personal accounts, Sky Club memberships, Silver Medallion certificates.

**Mid-Market + Enterprise (unified message):**
"Tu equipo viaja, y Delta trabaja para que llegue antes, mejor sentado, y con un plan cuando algo falla."
→ English: *"Your team travels, and Delta works to get them there sooner, better seated, and with a plan when things go wrong."*
→ **The pivot:** Corporate Priority — not a mileage program but a systematic preferential treatment that activates automatically on every booking through the Corporate Ticket Designator. Six concrete benefits: preferred seating, priority boarding, upgrade queue preference, standby priority, rebooking priority, overbooking protection. Extends to AF, KLM, Aeromexico, Virgin Atlantic, LATAM.
→ **Mid vs Enterprise difference:** Not the type of benefit but the depth — Mid-Market (MSA) gets negotiated fares + account manager; Enterprise (CSA) adds global coverage, unused ticket transfer, automatic reporting, and 24/7 Corporate Solutions.

## Critical clarifications for copy accuracy

These are commonly misunderstood aspects that the web must get right. Incorrect assumptions here will erode trust with knowledgeable users.

### 1. SkyMiles for Business tiers (Member → Plus → Elite)

These are the **company's program tiers**, completely separate from Medallion (which is the employee's personal status). The progression:

- **Member** — Free on enrollment. Portal access + offers. But no mile earning. Just the entry door.
- **Plus** — Activates at 5 registered travelers + $5,000 eligible flight revenue/year. This is when the company starts earning real miles (up to 10 miles/$).
- **Elite** — Same 5 travelers but $300,000/year. Adds +15% bonus on all miles + dedicated account manager.

The upgrade is automatic — Delta detects threshold crossing. No re-enrollment needed.

### 2. Employee earning in SkyMiles for Business

Yes, the employee earns personal miles. This is mechanically real, not marketing language. When an employee flies with the Business ID attached:
- Company account earns miles on fare base (program rates)
- Employee's personal SkyMiles account earns their own miles + MQDs at their normal Medallion multiplier

Neither account reduces the other. Delta absorbs the cost of double crediting.

### 3. What the company can do with its miles (exactly 4 uses)

1. **Transfer to employee personal SkyMiles accounts** — immediate, unidirectional, irreversible
2. **Redeem as eCredits** — ~1.5–2¢/mile, processed in <4h, applicable to future tickets (limit: 1 eCredit per ticket). Since June 2025, also redeemable via GDS/agencies.
3. **Buy Sky Club memberships** for individual employees (annual)
4. **Grant Silver Medallion certificates** — 12 months of Silver status for a specific employee, processed in 1 day

What the company cannot do: book award flights directly, accumulate indefinitely without activity, or use miles for Basic Economy tickets.

### 4. Card ownership — the most misunderstood aspect

Delta Amex business cards are held by an **individual** (business owner/officer), not the company. Applied for with personal credit + business EIN. Up to 99 employee cards at no extra cost. But **all miles from all employee card spending flow to the primary cardholder's personal SkyMiles account** — not to the company account, not to the employee's account.

**The card system and SkyMiles for Business are completely parallel.** Card = personal miles for the owner. Program = company miles from flights. Neither feeds the other. The web must make this crystal clear, especially on `/cards/compare/`.

### 5. Minimum spend thresholds

- **To join SkyMiles for Business:** $0. Any company can register free.
- **To start earning miles (Plus tier):** 5 unique travelers + $5,000 in eligible flight revenue per program year (Feb 1 – Jan 31). Eligible = base fare excluding taxes/fees. Basic Economy excluded.
- **To reach Elite:** 5 travelers + $300,000 eligible flight revenue.
- **Context:** Average US corporate round-trip ticket = $2,500–$10,000. Five travelers doing 4 trips/year at $500 base fare = $10,000 — already above the Plus threshold.

## Competitive messaging hooks (use in copy)

From `04-competitive-analysis.md` — these are ready-to-use angles:

- **Against United (SME):** "Most airlines require $50,000 minimum. Delta starts at $5,000."
- **United PerksPlus migration:** "United PerksPlus is no longer accepting new enrollments. Delta SkyMiles for Business is free to join today."
- **Against AA (earning):** "AAdvantage Business earns a flat 1 mile per dollar. Delta earns up to 10 miles per dollar on premium non-hub routes."
- **Against United (status):** "United Premier 1K requires 60 flights or $28K spend. Delta Diamond requires $28K — period. No flight count."
- **Individual program:** "Delta is the only major US airline with a dedicated program for individual business travelers."
- **SMB threshold:** "Free enrollment. $5,000 minimum to start earning — the lowest threshold of any major US airline business program."

### Individual Business Traveler
- Lead with: personal freedom, zero friction
- Pain point: "Flying for work shouldn't mean sacrificing your personal rewards"
- Hero copy: "Travel for work. Earn for you."
- CTA: "Get it free" (emphasis on free + instant)

### Small Business (SMB)
- Lead with: the double-earn mechanic — it's the unique insight
- Pain point: "Your company pays for the flight. Why isn't the company earning too?"
- Hero copy: "Your company earns miles. Your employees earn miles. Same flight."
- CTA: "Get started free — takes 3 minutes"

### Mid-Market (MSA)
- Lead with: travel manager efficiency + Corporate Priority
- Pain point: "Managing 50+ travelers without a dedicated system is chaos"
- Hero copy: "Managed travel, built around your team"
- CTA: "Contact sales" — with "response within 4 hours" promise

### Large Enterprise (CSA)
- Lead with: global coverage + full corporate infrastructure
- Pain point: "Your travelers need priority everywhere, not just US domestic"
- Hero copy: "Corporate Priority extends wherever your business does"
- CTA: "Contact sales" — with enterprise credibility signals

## The quiz — plain language labels

**Step 1 labels (not jargon):**
- "Just me" (not "1 traveler")
- "A small team: 2–50 people"
- "A mid-size company: 50–300 people"
- "A large enterprise: 300+ people"

**Step 2 labels (outcome-focused):**
- "Personal travel perks — no company enrollment needed"
- "My company earning miles on every flight we book"
- "Negotiated fares and priority service for my team"
- "A fully managed travel program with dedicated support"

**Result page:**
- "Based on your profile, you're a [SEGMENT] match"
- Emphasize what THEY said, reflected back to them
- Show the financial implication immediately

---

## SEO content rules

- Primary keyword in H1 (exact or near-match)
- Related keywords in H2s
- FAQ sections target long-tail voice-search queries
- All program pages: include comparison context (vs competitors)
- Card pages: include "2025" or "2026" in title for recency signals
